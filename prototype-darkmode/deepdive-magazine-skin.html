The "Notebook" as a New Content Format

Johnson's central claim is that the notebook — a curated collection of sources bundled with a conversational AI interface — is emerging as a genuinely new medium, on par with the article or the book rather than a feature bolted onto either.

The argument turns on a shift in what is being sold. A book delivers a finished, linear narrative; the author's sources are invisible and inert. A notebook instead delivers the source material itself, wrapped in an interface that lets the reader interrogate it — ask questions, request summaries, trace a claim back to its origin, or explore the corpus non-linearly. The author's role moves from "narrator of conclusions" to "curator of a queryable knowledge base."

Johnson grounds this in Google's "featured notebooks" pilot, run with publishing partners — The Atlantic, The Economist, and the Royal Society among them — each packaging high-quality sources and artifacts for a specific audience.

  • Financial analysis — a notebook bundling the earnings reports of large public companies, where an analyst can ask for a cross-company summary instead of reading each filing.
  • Academic study — the complete works of Shakespeare paired with a modern-English translation layer, queryable line by line.
  • Niche/hobby use — managing a Dungeons & Dragons campaign, where the model generates narrative recaps of past sessions on demand.

Why the Interface Changes the Economics

Bundling sources with a query layer collapses the distance between a reader's question and an authoritative answer, and that collapse is what gives the format its commercial pull.

In the old model, the cost of going from "I have a question" to "I have a grounded answer" was paid in reading time. The notebook shifts that cost to up-front curation: the publisher does the work of assembling and vetting the corpus once, and every reader thereafter pays only the marginal cost of a query. For high-value, reference-heavy domains — law, finance, medicine, scholarship — that trade is strongly favorable.

"You're not buying the conclusions anymore. You're buying access to the questions you didn't know to ask." — paraphrasing Johnson, 18:40

The Curation-to-Query Pipeline

Underneath the product is a repeatable pipeline that turns a pile of documents into an interrogable notebook — and understanding it explains both the format's strengths and its failure modes.

Curated sources (vetted by publisher)
  ↓ (ingest + chunk)
Embedded knowledge base
  ↓ (reader asks a question)
Retrieval over the corpus
  ↓ (grounded generation, with citations)
Answer + narrative summary
  ↓ (reader follows a citation)
Back to the original source

The citation loop at the bottom is the part Johnson stresses: because every generated answer can be traced back to a vetted source, the notebook inherits the credibility of its corpus rather than the open-ended unreliability of a general chatbot. The quality of the output is bounded by the quality of the curation — which is precisely why publishers, not the model, are the gatekeepers.
